Executive Summary Show

Market Regime — Offensive Factors Rolling Over Together

This ChartList tracks five US equity factor ratios — growth versus value across large, mid, and small caps, high beta versus low volatility, and the momentum factor versus the core S&P 500. All five are expressions of the same underlying thing: risk appetite and offensive-factor leadership. As of the July 17 close, every one of them is below its 50-day average and rolling over, which is an unusually uniform message.

None of the five ratios is in a bullish posture. Four sit in the caution bucket — below the 50-day average with deteriorating momentum but still above a rising 200-day average — while mid-cap growth/value has already broken below both averages into an outright downtrend. There is no offsetting strength inside this list: the factor complex is shifting from offense toward defense in unison.

Leadership Read

The growth-over-value trade is unwinding fastest where it ran hottest. Mid-cap growth/value (MDYG:MDYV) is the most broken at 1.127, below both moving averages with PPO at -0.910. Large-cap growth/value (SPYG:SPYV) closed at 1.88, testing its 200-day average. Small-cap growth/value (SLYG:SLYV) is the relative holdout at 1.055, still clinging to its 200-day after a sharp reversal from the early-July high near 1.101.

The two pure risk-appetite gauges tell the same story with even sharper momentum. High beta versus low volatility (SPHB:SPLV) at 1.841 and the momentum factor versus the S&P 500 (MTUM:SPY) at 0.406 posted the steepest PPO readings in the list, -1.473 and -1.187, each peeling back from an early-summer peak toward — but not yet through — its 200-day average.

Key Conclusion & Thesis

The consistent message is a risk-appetite cooldown: the market's offensive factors led into June and early July and are now correcting together. Because all five ratios remain above — or, for mid-caps, just below — their 200-day averages, this reads as a factor rotation and corrective pullback rather than a confirmed, durable regime change.

The tell to watch is the 200-day average on each ratio. Those lines are still rising, and holding them keeps the longer offensive trend alive; breaking them would argue that defensive, low-volatility, value leadership is taking over. This is a disciplined caution, not an all-clear and not yet a confirmed defensive regime.

Bottom Line

As of the July 17 close, StockCharts list 320 — Key Ratios: US Factors — is sending a single, consistent message: the offensive factors that led the market into early summer are correcting together. Growth is ceding to value, high beta to low volatility, and momentum to the core index, all at the same time.
