## Executive summary

### Overall market regime

The 37-chart collection presents a **constructive, risk-on market regime with narrowing-leadership and momentum risks**. The primary equity trend remains bullish across daily, weekly, and monthly time frames. SPY and the S&P 500 are above their major moving averages; the weekly PPO structure is positive; and the NYSE Composite remains above its rising 12- and 60-month averages. Credit spreads, VIX, and MOVE do not presently confirm a major financial-stress event.

The evidence therefore favors treating weakness as consolidation within an uptrend unless important support levels break. It does not support an immediate secular bear-market conclusion. At the same time, the charts do not describe a uniformly strong market in which all groups and internal indicators are advancing together.

### Page 1 — market health, breadth, and leadership

Page 1’s strongest bullish evidence comes from the primary SPY/SPX trend, calm high-yield credit, contained volatility, improving capitalization-tier advance-decline lines, and continued offensive leadership. High-beta and cyclical behavior is visible through discretionary versus staples, defensive-sector underperformance, improving small-cap relative strength, and exceptional semiconductor performance.

Breadth is positive but only moderately strong. Approximately 61% of S&P 500 stocks are above their 200-day averages and roughly 56% are above their 50-day averages. The S&P 500 Bullish Percent Index is above 50, but not near the powerful 70-plus region. These readings are sufficient to support the advance, but they do not constitute a broad participation thrust.

The weaker internals deserve attention. The Nasdaq 100 Bullish Percent Index is below 50 even as technology-heavy indexes remain strong. Chaikin Money Flow is failing to confirm SPY’s price strength, new-low readings have increased, and RSP/SPY remains below its 200-day average. Together, these charts show that headline indexes are healthier than some of the underlying participation measures.

Technology and semiconductors remain the market’s most important leadership engine. That is bullish while relative strength continues higher, but it creates concentration risk. The market would become more durable if equal weight, small caps, financials, industrials, and broader growth participation strengthened without requiring semiconductor leadership to weaken.

Gold, crude oil, and Bitcoin are technically weaker than equities. Gold and crude are testing important long-term moving-average areas, while Bitcoin remains below its 50- and 200-day averages. Their weakness does not currently invalidate the equity bull case, but it shows that risk appetite is selective rather than universal across assets.

### Page 2 — risk appetite and long-term confirmation

Page 2 broadly confirms a risk-on environment. SPHB/SPLV shows high-beta stocks decisively outperforming low-volatility stocks, while SPY/TLT confirms that equities remain preferable to long-duration Treasury bonds on a relative-strength basis. These are among the ChartList’s clearest bullish allocation signals.

Growth leadership is less straightforward. The pure-growth versus pure-value ratio is breaking to a strong relative high, but the broader IWF/IWD ratio remains below its 50-day average. This suggests that the most aggressive growth cohort is thriving while the wider growth universe has not fully confirmed the move.

U.S. relative leadership is a notable weakness. SPY/ACWI is below its 50- and 200-day averages, indicating that U.S. stocks are lagging the global equity benchmark. The overall market can remain bullish while international equities outperform, but this chart challenges the assumption that U.S. equities should automatically receive all incremental equity allocation.

The weekly S&P 500 remains firmly above its 10- and 40-week averages, and weekly PPO is positive. However, PPO and RSI display divergence risk: price has reached higher highs without proportionately stronger momentum highs. Divergence alone is not a sell signal, but it means the advance is becoming less efficient and more vulnerable to a correction if price support fails.

The monthly NYSE Composite, PPO, and Coppock Curve remain constructive. These long-duration charts provide the strongest evidence that the secular trend is intact. A monthly close below the 12-month average combined with a bearish PPO crossover would be needed before the long-term message became materially defensive.

### Portfolio interpretation

The combined evidence supports a **constructive but disciplined** posture:

- Maintain respect for the established equity uptrend.
- Prefer controlled pullbacks or confirmed breakouts over chasing extended semiconductor and high-beta strength.
- Watch whether equal weight and broader breadth begin confirming the headline indexes.
- Recognize that international equities currently have better relative momentum than U.S. equities.
- Treat a simultaneous deterioration in price, credit, volatility, and breadth as more important than weakness in any single indicator.

### Key confirmation and invalidation levels

The bullish case would strengthen if the S&P 500 clears **7,500–7,600** with improving breadth, RSP/SPY reclaims its 200-day average, the Nasdaq 100 Bullish Percent Index moves above 50, and Chaikin Money Flow returns to positive territory.

The first warnings would be an S&P 500 break below approximately **7,460**, VIX holding above **20**, SPHB/SPLV falling below approximately **1.90**, SPY/TLT losing approximately **8.55**, or the weekly S&P 500 closing below its 10-week average.

A more serious risk-off regime would require multiple confirmations: the S&P 500 losing its 50-day average near **7,328**, widening high-yield spreads, VIX moving toward **28–30**, renewed MOVE stress, deteriorating equal-weight and small-cap relative strength, and eventual weakness in the NYSE Composite’s monthly trend indicators.

# Combined market interpretation

## Trend

The trend evidence is bullish across daily, weekly, and monthly horizons. Short-term momentum is less forceful than the long-term structure, but the charts do not yet show a confirmed primary trend reversal.

## Breadth

Breadth is positive but uneven. Most capitalization-tier advance-decline lines are healthy, and a majority of S&P 500 stocks are above major moving averages. However, the Nasdaq 100 Bullish Percent Index, new-low behavior, Chaikin Money Flow, and RSP/SPY show that participation is not uniformly strong.

## Leadership

Semiconductors and technology dominate. Small caps and industrials are improving, which is encouraging, but equal weight has not conclusively escaped its longer-term relative downtrend. The market would become healthier if leadership broadened without semiconductor strength collapsing.

## Risk appetite

High beta versus low volatility, discretionary versus staples, defensive-sector underperformance, and stocks versus bonds all support a risk-on conclusion. These are meaningful confirmations because they reflect investor preference rather than index price alone.

## Macro and cross-asset evidence

Credit and volatility remain calm, while the 10-year Treasury yield is rising. Gold, oil, and Bitcoin are technically weak. A gradual decline in commodity pressure can help equities, but continued commodity weakness should be watched for possible growth implications.

## Geographic allocation

U.S. equities are not leading global equities. The SPY/ACWI breakdown is one of the clearest challenges to an exclusively U.S.-centric allocation thesis.

## Sentiment and positioning

Retail sentiment remains skeptical, while professional manager exposure is high. This combination is neither straightforwardly bullish nor bearish: skepticism leaves potential buying power, but high manager exposure creates vulnerability if price support fails.

# Final assessment

The ChartList’s dominant message is **bullish, risk-on, and leadership-dependent**. The primary trend remains intact, credit and volatility are benign, and multiple relative-strength measures favor equities and aggressive exposure. The long-term NYSE Composite charts reinforce that the secular advance remains in place.

The market is not internally perfect. Technology and semiconductors are carrying a disproportionate share of leadership, Nasdaq breadth is weaker than its price index, money flow has softened, and equal weight has not fully confirmed a broad new leadership regime. U.S. stocks are also lagging global equities.

The most reasonable technical posture is therefore constructive without complacency: respect the uptrend, avoid interpreting every pullback as a new bear market, but demand breadth and momentum confirmation before treating an index breakout as exceptionally durable.
