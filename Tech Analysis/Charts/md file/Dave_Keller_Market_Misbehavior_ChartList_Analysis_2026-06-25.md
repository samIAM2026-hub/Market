## Executive summary

### Market regime: constructive, but now under tactical stress

The June 25 StockCharts refresh shifts the market read from “clean risk-on” to **constructive but tactically vulnerable**. The primary trend is still positive, but the short-term tape is no longer giving the market a free pass.

The S&P 500 closed at **7,357.49**, almost exactly on its 50-day average near **7,356.87**, and below the 21-day EMA near **7,424.68**. SPY’s weekly chart still holds above its 13-, 21-, and 34-week EMAs, so the primary uptrend is intact. But SPY has slipped below its 5-week EMA and short-term PPO has turned negative. That combination says the market is testing support rather than simply pausing in a smooth advance.

The strongest bullish evidence is breadth. The NYSE, large-cap, mid-cap, and small-cap advance-decline lines remain above rising 50-day averages. Around **62.8%** of S&P 500 stocks are above their 200-day averages, and around **64.2%** are above their 50-day averages. RSP/SPY has moved back above both its 50- and 200-day moving averages, and IWM/SPY is also above both key averages. In other words, the average stock is not falling apart underneath the index. Breadth is the main reason this still looks like a correction risk inside an uptrend rather than a confirmed bearish regime shift.

The weakest internal evidence is concentrated in Nasdaq/growth breadth and money flow. The Nasdaq 100 Bullish Percent Index is near **37**, clearly below the neutral 50 line. SPY Chaikin Money Flow has turned negative near **-0.127**. Broad growth versus value has weakened, even though pure growth and semiconductors remain strong. That is an important distinction: the market still has pockets of powerful leadership, but the leadership stack is not uniformly healthy.

Credit and volatility are not confirming panic. High-yield spreads remain calm, MOVE is low and falling, and VIX is still below 20. This matters because major equity breakdowns usually become more dangerous when credit, volatility, price, and breadth deteriorate together. As of the June 25 charts, that full confirmation is not present. The risk is real, but not yet systemic.

### Leadership read

Leadership is mixed and more nuanced than a simple “tech up, everything else down” story. Semiconductors remain the strongest leadership group by far, with SMH/SPY still in a powerful relative uptrend. Technology relative strength also remains constructive. At the same time, small caps, equal weight, industrials, financials, and materials have improved, which supports the broadening argument.

The offset is that broad growth leadership is not healthy. IWF/IWD is below its 50-day average, communications and consumer discretionary relative strength remain weak, and discretionary versus staples has rolled over. So the better description is **selective broadening with concentrated semiconductor leadership**, not broad, synchronized risk-on.

### Cross-asset message

Cross-asset charts reinforce the selective tone. Stocks still have a constructive longer-term setup versus bonds, but SPY/TLT has fallen below its 50-day average, which weakens the tactical allocation signal. The U.S. dollar is firm, while gold, crude oil, and Bitcoin are technically weak. That is not the signature of broad cross-asset risk appetite. It is a market where equity breadth is improving in some places, but commodities and crypto are not confirming the same enthusiasm.

### Trading interpretation

The dashboard argues for a **disciplined bullish bias with tighter risk control**. It does not argue for abandoning the equity uptrend, because breadth, credit, small caps, equal weight, high beta, semiconductors, and long-term NYSE indicators remain constructive. But it also does not support complacency, because the S&P 500 is sitting on the 50-day average, money flow is negative, VIX is close to 20, Nasdaq breadth is weak, and professional exposure is crowded.

The immediate decision zone is **7,350–7,360** on the S&P 500. A successful hold and rebound through the 21-day EMA near **7,425** would restore a cleaner bullish posture. A decisive break below the 50-day average, especially if VIX holds above **20** and SPY CMF remains negative, would shift the report toward a deeper correction warning.

### Key confirmation and invalidation levels

The bullish case improves if the S&P 500 reclaims the 21-day EMA near **7,425**, then clears **7,500–7,600** with improving breadth, positive SPY money flow, and VIX failing below 20.

The tactical caution case remains active while the S&P 500 sits near the 50-day average and SPY remains below its 5-week EMA.

The bearish/correction case strengthens if the S&P 500 closes decisively below the 50-day average, VIX holds above **20**, SPY/TLT remains below its 50-day average, and RSP/SPY or IWM/SPY lose their reclaimed moving-average support.

# Combined market interpretation

## 1. Price trend: primary uptrend, short-term stress test

The trend structure is split by time frame. The weekly and monthly charts still support a bullish primary regime: SPY remains above its 13-, 21-, and 34-week EMAs, the S&P 500 weekly chart remains above its 40-week average, and the NYSE Composite remains above its 12- and 60-month averages.

The daily chart is where the problem lives. The S&P 500 closed at **7,357.49**, sitting almost exactly on the 50-day average and below the 21-day EMA. That is not a broken primary trend, but it is a tactical stress test. If the 50-day average holds, this can remain a normal pullback. If it fails and the rebound attempts are weak, the market shifts from “constructive consolidation” to “active correction risk.”

## 2. Breadth: stronger than the price action suggests

Breadth is the most constructive part of the June 25 dashboard. The NYSE, large-cap, mid-cap, and small-cap advance-decline lines are still above rising 50-day averages. S&P 500 participation is also healthy: about **62.8%** of members are above their 200-day averages and about **64.2%** are above their 50-day averages.

This is not the internal profile of a market that has already broadly broken down. It is the profile of a market where the headline index is testing support while many underlying participation measures are still holding. That breadth support is why the report should not become outright bearish yet.

The caveat is Nasdaq breadth. The Nasdaq 100 Bullish Percent Index near **37** is meaningfully weak. So the breadth message is not universally bullish: broad S&P and NYSE breadth look constructive, while Nasdaq 100 participation looks fragile.

## 3. Leadership: broadening exists, but leadership quality is uneven

The leadership picture is no longer a simple narrow mega-cap story. Equal-weight S&P relative strength has reclaimed both its 50- and 200-day averages. Small caps have also moved above their 50- and 200-day relative averages. Industrials, financials, and materials have improved from their June lows.

That is genuine broadening, and it is one of the strongest bullish changes from the prior report.

At the same time, leadership quality is uneven. Semiconductors remain exceptionally strong versus the S&P 500, but that relative trend is extended. Technology is still constructive, but communications and discretionary relative strength are weak. Broad growth versus value has deteriorated even though pure growth remains strong. The result is a market with selective broadening but not synchronized leadership.

## 4. Risk appetite: positive in some equity ratios, weaker in cross-asset confirmation

High beta still leads low volatility, which is an important bullish risk-appetite signal. Small caps outperforming large caps and equal weight outperforming cap weight also support risk appetite inside equities.

But other risk-appetite gauges have cooled. Discretionary versus staples has rolled over. SPY/TLT is below its 50-day average, so stocks versus bonds no longer gives a clean tactical risk-on signal. SPY Chaikin Money Flow is negative, which means accumulation is no longer confirming price.

Risk appetite is therefore best described as **mixed but not broken**. Equity internals still show appetite for risk, but cross-asset and money-flow evidence are less supportive.

## 5. Macro and cross-asset evidence: no panic, but no broad enthusiasm either

Credit is the most important stabilizer. High-yield spreads remain calm around **2.71**, and MOVE is low near **67.10**. Those charts do not confirm a systemic stress event.

The weaker side of the macro dashboard is commodities and crypto. Gold is below its 21-day, 50-day, and 200-day moving averages. Crude oil is below its 200-day average and deeply below its 50-day average. Bitcoin is below both its 50- and 200-day averages. The U.S. dollar is firm. This combination argues against broad cross-asset risk enthusiasm.

## 6. Sentiment and positioning: not euphoric broadly, but manager exposure is crowded

AAII sentiment is positive but not extreme. NAAIM is the bigger issue: exposure is near **98.6**, which means professional managers are already heavily allocated to equities.

High exposure is not automatically bearish in a strong trend, but it lowers the market’s margin for error. If price starts failing support, there may be less incremental buying power available from already-exposed managers.

## 7. Geographic allocation: U.S. relative leadership remains soft

SPY/ACWI remains below both its 50- and 200-day averages. This says U.S. equities are still lagging global equities on a relative basis.

That does not mean U.S. equities are bearish outright. It means the relative-strength argument for concentrating only in U.S. equities is weaker than it was in stronger U.S.-leadership regimes.

# Final assessment

The June 25 update is a genuine change in tone. The market is no longer giving a clean, low-friction risk-on message. The S&P 500 is testing its 50-day average, short-term momentum has weakened, SPY money flow is negative, VIX is pressing toward 20, and Nasdaq 100 breadth is poor.

But the dashboard is not bearish enough to call a full regime break. Breadth remains too constructive, credit remains too calm, MOVE remains too low, and long-term trend indicators remain too healthy for that conclusion. Equal weight and small caps are actually improving, which is the opposite of what one would expect in a fully broken market.

The best summary is: **primary uptrend intact, tactical risk elevated, breadth still supportive, leadership selective, risk controls required**.

If the S&P 500 holds **7,350–7,360** and reclaims **7,425**, the pullback can be treated as a normal consolidation. If the 50-day average fails while VIX holds above **20** and CMF remains negative, the market would move into a deeper correction-warning phase.
