Verified facts The federal-funds target range is 3.50%–3.75%. After today's CPI, futures implied roughly an 83% chance of no hike at the July 28–29 meeting and about 17% for a 25 bp hike; Reuters reported only ~10% immediately after the release and ~60% odds of a hike by September. Reuters / CME-implied pricing.

Guidance: Chair Warsh said the Fed has “no tolerance” for persistently high inflation but deliberately avoided signaling the next move. Governor Waller had warned that several months of cooler inflation would be needed. Today's release lowered the expected path by roughly one quarter-point at the front of the curve relative to pre-CPI pricing; compared with a week ago, a July hike moved from a live risk to a tail risk.

Six-to-twelve months: markets are no longer pricing cuts as the central case; the live debate is hold versus one or more hikes. A reasonable market-consistent description is 0–2 hikes over 6–12 months, with the range unusually wide because energy and tariff shocks can reaccelerate inflation while payroll growth is weakening.

5. Bank of Canada Analysis

Verified facts The overnight target is 2.25%. All 36 economists in Reuters' July 13 poll expected a hold on July 15; a majority expected no change through at least July 2027. Money markets nevertheless assigned roughly an 80% chance of one 25 bp hike by year-end. Reuters preview.

The BoC's June guidance was conditional: look through a temporary energy spike, but react if inflation broadens; renewed U.S. trade restrictions could instead require easing. The April MPR projected 1.2% 2026 growth and 2.3% inflation. Tomorrow's MPR will update both.

  • Inflation: May CPI 3.2% y/y, led by gasoline; CPI excluding gasoline 2.2%; core measures are roughly 2%–2.7%.
  • Jobs/wages: June employment +18,000; unemployment 6.5%; participation 65.0%; wages +3.3% y/y. Monthly gains were modest and manufacturing lost 17,000 jobs.

  • Business/productivity: business capital investment fell 0.7% in Q1, a fifth consecutive decline; weak investment continues to cap productivity.
  • Fiscal/trade: public-sector activity and defence spending support demand, while USMCA review and U.S. tariffs constrain manufacturing and investment.

19. General Currency-Conversion Framework

This section is educational and addresses hypothetical USD-to-CAD conversions. It does not assume any holdings, need to transact, amount, account, or personal circumstances. Conversion and speculative trading are different: conversion manages timing and operational needs; trading intentionally takes price risk.

At 1.4066, the rate remains historically elevated versus the 2025 year-end reference, but is roughly 90 pips below the prior close and directly ahead of the BoC. That combination generally favors staging rather than an all-or-nothing timing decision.

Illustrative approachHypothetical frameworkTrade-off
1. ConservativeConvert 60%–80% of a hypothetical need now; reserve 20%–40% for after BoC. Use dates, not forecasts, to ensure operational certainty.Lowest timing risk; gives up some upside if USD/CAD rebounds
2. Balanced stagedConvert 30%–40% near current levels; 30% near 1.4115–1.4180; remaining 30% if price falls through 1.4035/1.4000 or by a fixed deadline.Reduces regret and preserves optionality
3. Aggressive rate-seekingConvert 10%–20% now; place hypothetical limit tranches around 1.4150 and 1.4240; protect the deadline with a trigger if 1.4000 breaks.Highest chance of a better rate, but greatest risk of receiving fewer CAD
  • If USD/CAD rises sharply, avoid chasing the first spike; staged limits can monetize strength.
  • If it falls sharply, a precommitted floor/date tranche prevents indefinite waiting.
  • Because BoC starts at 6:45 a.m. Vancouver time, avoid relying on manual execution during the first volatile minutes.
  • A limit order can suit a non-urgent hypothetical conversion, but may never fill and can incur spreads/fees.
  • Judge the all-in customer rate—including bank spread and fees—not only institutional spot.
