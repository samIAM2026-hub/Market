1. Executive Summary

  • Dominant narrative: June producer prices unexpectedly fell, adding to Tuesday's softer CPI and reducing pressure for an immediate Federal Reserve increase.
  • The S&P 500 rose 0.38%, Nasdaq Composite 0.62%, Dow 0.29% and Russell 2000 0.39%; all four major indexes gained, but technology leadership split sharply.
  • Final-demand PPI fell 0.3% m/m versus a flat consensus and slowed to 5.5% y/y; energy drove the goods decline, while services rose 0.2%. BLS
  • Two-year and ten-year Treasury yields fell to roughly 4.14% and 4.55%–4.56%. Market-implied July-hike odds declined to about 10% from nearly 42% Monday. AP
  • Investor mood: moderately risk-on, not euphoric. Hyperscalers, asset managers and custody banks rose; Micron, Elevance Health and J&J fell.
  • Global reinforcement: ASML raised its 2026 outlook on AI demand and South Korea's KOSPI surged 6.2%, but China's growth slowed and Brent remained near $85.
  • Takeaway: evidence strengthened the “growth intact, inflation easing” interpretation, yet oil and firm service-price margins leave inflation unresolved.

Growth and value both contributed: internet platforms and financials rose, while healthcare lagged. Small caps kept pace with the S&P, arguing against a purely megacap rally, but a weaker Nasdaq-100 and Micron's 8.2% fall show concentration within technology.

3. Market Drivers

  1. Producer-price relief: the 0.3% PPI decline versus a flat consensus reduced the probability that wholesale inflation would force a near-term Fed response; the front end of the Treasury curve reacted most.
  2. AI demand held, leadership rotated: ASML lifted its annual outlook after €9.3 billion of sales, supporting hyperscalers; memory shares fell, leaving the Nasdaq-100 weaker than the Composite.
  3. Financial earnings broadened profits: BlackRock, BNY and Morgan Stanley reported strong fees, assets and capital-markets revenue, supporting financials without requiring higher yields.
  4. Fed communication validated patience, not easing certainty: Williams said inflation appears to have peaked; Warsh stressed data imperfections and persistent-inflation discipline without a meeting signal.
  5. Oil and healthcare capped enthusiasm: Brent held near $85, while Elevance and J&J fell despite beats or higher guidance.

Versus expectations: PPI was cooler, Empire manufacturing stronger, and AI/financial results generally better. Oil was the main macro contradiction.

5. Rates, Bonds, and the U.S. Dollar

The two-year yield fell roughly 7 bp to 4.14%; the ten-year fell 3–4 bp to about 4.55%–4.56%. The 2s10s curve steepened to roughly +42 bp from about +37—a bull steepening, meaning yields fell while shorter maturities rallied more.

Softer PPI reduced immediate-policy risk, most visible at two years. The ten-year fell less because longer maturities retain oil, fiscal-supply and inflation-risk compensation. Lower yields helped long-duration equities but did not erase company-specific disappointment.

DXY slipped about 0.4% to 100.52 as the front-end advantage narrowed, supporting gold and marginally easing global financial conditions. No material same-day coupon auction or verified credit dislocation drove the move; same-day credit spreads were unavailable, so no fresh credit signal is inferred.

8. Market Breadth and Leadership

All four major indexes rose, and Russell 2000 +0.39% matched S&P +0.38%—better participation than a cap-weighted-only rally. The Dow gained despite healthcare weakness, and the Composite outperformed on platforms.

Participation remained uneven. The Nasdaq-100 was down about 0.3% in late data while the Composite rose; Micron fell sharply; several platforms delivered much growth contribution. Final equal-weight S&P and exchange advance/decline data were unavailable.

Interpretation: moderately risk-on and rotational—not defensive, but not a uniform beta chase. Falling yields supported duration, financial operating leverage was rewarded, and company cost problems were punished.

9. Cross-Asset Confirmation

Treasuries, DXY and VIX confirmed the equity narrative: yields and dollar fell; VIX dropped about 0.8 point to 15.67. Gold's small gain fit lower yield/dollar pressure, not acute flight-to-safety demand.

Energy contradicted clean disinflation. Brent settled $84.95 and WTI traded around $80 after crude inventories fell about 1.7M barrels. June PPI benefited from gasoline −12%, but July commodities were reversing some relief. Natural gas rose modestly and copper was flat, so no generalized supply shock or global boom was confirmed.

Same-day credit-spread data were unavailable; lower VIX and higher equities should not be called credit-confirmed. Bitcoin was little changed and immaterial.

10. Global and Geopolitical Context

KOSPI rose 6.2%, Taiwan about 2% and Nikkei about 1.5%, reinforcing global AI capex. ASML transmitted this into U.S. platforms and equipment: leading-edge demand supports cloud infrastructure even when chip subsectors diverge.

China sent the opposite cyclical signal. Q2 GDP slowed to 4.3% from 5.0%; Hong Kong rose 1.4%, Shanghai fell 0.3%. The U.S. read-through was softer industrial-material and machinery demand, consistent with flat copper and weaker Caterpillar. AP

Europe was roughly steady: ASML enthusiasm offset oil/Middle East uncertainty. Hormuz access, tanker insurance and strike risk can lift crude, freight and inflation expectations, then Treasury yields and rate-sensitive equities.

Key Event Preview — Retail Sales

The GDP control group matters more than gasoline. A broad beat lifts growth, yields and USD, favoring banks, discretionary and industrials while pressuring duration. In line preserves soft landing. A miss led by real discretionary weakness lowers growth/yields; defensives may outperform.

Key Event Preview — Import Prices, Housing and Production

Import prices test tariff, dollar and energy pass-through. Hot nonfuel prices revive Fed risk; cool validates disinflation. Starts/permits separate current building from pipeline, while production tests whether Empire strength is national. Strong activity with tame imports is the best mix; strong/hot is hawkish; broad weakness shifts focus to growth.

Key Event Preview — Michigan Sentiment

One- and five-year inflation expectations matter most. Higher expectations after oil lift yields/USD and favor energy/financials. Stable expectations with better sentiment support consumer cyclicals. Lower sentiment and expectations ease rates but question demand.

13. Bottom Line

Regime: moderately risk-on, disinflation-sensitive and earnings-selective. The move has more foundation than a single-stock rally because all major indexes rose, short yields fell and growth stayed positive. Durability depends on retail/import prices, whether AI demand broadens, and whether oil stops reversing June relief.

Unresolved: Are services slowing enough for patience? Can consumer strength persist without price pressure? Will Hormuz re-enter expectations? Do AI-capex signals translate into broad profits?
