The Value Line View

Growth is cooling. Q1 GDP was revised down to 1.6% (from 2.0%) on slower inventory restocking and weaker consumer demand. Reaccelerating inflation is eroding purchasing power, and higher mortgage rates are hurting residential construction.

But pockets of strength remain. May ISM manufacturing rose to 54.0% (up over a full point), and April durable-goods orders jumped 7.9% — more than double expectations. The question is durability, given higher energy prices.

Earnings carry the market. S&P 500 profit growth is seen averaging ~25% this year, with AI-infrastructure beneficiaries accounting for nearly half the Index's gains — a support for stocks, but also a concentration risk if AI spending fails to convert into recurring profits.

Conclusion: Major averages hit record highs in May on strong earnings. The economy is more mixed — softening consumer spending, elevated input costs, fading fiscal stimulus — but near-term profit growth should continue to support equities.

The Iran War and the Green Energy Transition — Nathaniel Eakman, Analyst

After U.S. strikes on Iran in late February closed shipping through the Strait of Hormuz and sent oil prices surging, a popular thesis held that the war would accelerate the move to green energy. So far, it has: demand for Chinese solar panels doubled in March, and EV and battery exports hit record highs.

Why that's surprising: historically, conflict slows the energy transition. A 2025 study in Renewable and Sustainable Energy Reviews found greater geopolitical risk has a negative effect on renewable development — crises usually push countries to secure more fossil fuels (e.g., China ramping coal after Russia invaded Ukraine), not fewer.

1 · A tipping point on cost & speed

Renewables keep getting cheaper to build and run, and projects already in progress can be finished fast — reducing the need for short-term fossil-fuel fixes. Countries early in their transition remain the most vulnerable to conflict-driven setbacks.

2 · A new geopolitical landscape

Unlike 2022, the U.S. is now a direct participant in the conflict and has stepped back from climate and security commitments. Energy independence is becoming a priority — and for countries without domestic fossil fuels, that means renewables.

3 · China is the winner

China leads in producing green technology and in mining/processing the rare earths it requires — so even relocated production stays dependent on Chinese supply chains. A durable shift could alter the balance of international power.

Remaining obstacles

Battery-technology limits, reliability questions for some renewable sources, and the continued need for fossil fuels in aviation and fertilizer production. The key question: is this boost a permanent change or a temporary aberration?
