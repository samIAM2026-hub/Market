Tax note for RRSP holders: Canadian-listed ETFs that hold US equities still pay 15% US dividend withholding tax, which is NOT recoverable inside an RRSP. To avoid this, hold US-domiciled ETFs (VOO, VTI, SPY) directly in your RRSP using USD. The tax saving of ~0.30–0.40%/year can exceed the currency conversion cost over time. In TFSA and non-registered accounts, the withholding tax applies regardless — so the choice of Canadian-listed vs US-listed matters less there. HXS exception: Swap-based structure means no US dividends flow through the ETF, so no withholding tax in any account type. 🚩 Red Flags — What to Avoid

AUM Below $30–50M CAD
Closure risk increases sharply. Low AUM = poor liquidity, wide spreads, and potential forced liquidation. Minimum viable AUM for a core holding: $50M CAD. Below $30M: avoid entirely for long-term portfolios.
Bid-Ask Spread >25–30 bps
Acceptable for niche tactical ETFs, but avoid as core holdings. Every time you buy or sell, you lose half the spread. On a $100K position, 30 bps = $300 loss per round-trip trade.
Unproven Issuer / No Track Record
Stick to established issuers (iShares, BMO, Vanguard, Global X, TD, CI). New entrants without institutional backing increase operational risk, tracking error, and closure probability.
Swap-Based ETFs in Non-Reg Accounts
ETFs like some Global X swap-based products defer income but carry counterparty risk (the swap counterparty could default). Complex tax treatment outside registered accounts. Consult a tax advisor.
Leveraged/Inverse ETFs for Long-Term
Volatility decay destroys returns in leveraged products held over months. These are daily rebalancing instruments designed for short-term tactical use only. Never mistake them for index ETFs.
High MER Without Justification
Covered call ETFs (0.65–0.91% MER) are justified for income. But paying 0.65%+ for a passive sector ETF with 15 holdings is unjustifiable when broad market ETFs deliver similar sector exposure at 0.06%.
