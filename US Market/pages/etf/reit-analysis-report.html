Are REITs attractive today versus broad equities? Forward opinion Selectively. After a multi-year de-rating driven by higher rates, listed REITs trade at meaningful discounts to private-market real estate value and at dividend yields well above their own history. That is a reasonable entry point for a diversifier, but with the 10-year Treasury at 4.48% and the REIT yield around 4%, REITs no longer offer the easy yield premium over bonds they did in the 2010s. The opportunity is in quality and sector selection, not the asset class as a blunt instrument.

How have REITs performed versus the S&P 500? Historical data Over the last 10 years REITs badly lagged (6.2% vs 12.9% annualized), over 20 years they lagged (7.1% vs 10.5%), and over 30 years they roughly matched but still trailed (9.5% vs 10.4%). REITs did win over 25 years (9.5% vs 7.9%) — but that window simply starts in 2000, right before the dot-com crash hammered the S&P. The honest read: over most long horizons REITs have not beaten the S&P 500; the 25-year win is a start-date artifact.

Best and worst sectors historically. Historical data The structural winners have been industrial/logistics, data centers, cell towers, self-storage and manufactured housing — sectors tied to e-commerce, the digital economy and pricing power. The structural laggards have been office, regional malls/weaker retail, and mortgage REITs, the last of which has produced near-zero 20-year total returns despite double-digit headline yields.

Income, diversification, inflation or total return? Forward opinion REITs are best understood as an income + diversification + inflation-sensitivity holding. They are not a reliable total-return enhancer over a portfolio that already owns the S&P 500. Their correlation to equities is high enough that the diversification is partial, but their cash-flow profile and real-asset linkage are genuinely different.

Domestic, U.S., global, or ETF? Product recommendation For a Canadian, the cleanest core is a low-cost broad U.S. REIT ETF (the U.S. is ~70% of the global listed market and far more diversified by sector than Canada), ideally held in an RRSP to avoid the 15% U.S. withholding tax. A Canadian-listed REIT ETF adds home-currency income but concentrates you in retail, residential and industrial with almost no data-center or tower exposure. Most investors are best served by broad ETFs, not single names.

Key risks: interest rates and refinancing (REITs are leveraged, rate-sensitive cash-flow vehicles), the real-estate cycle, sector-specific obsolescence (office, weak retail), valuation, and — critically for cross-border investors — tax treatment, since REIT distributions are mostly ordinary/non-eligible income and are taxed unfavourably outside registered accounts.

The investment case for REITs depends mainly on sector selection, valuation discipline, the path of long-term interest rates, and holding them in the right (tax-sheltered) account — not on any expectation that the asset class as a whole will outperform the S&P 500.

SECTION 2REIT Basics: What REITs Are

A REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust) is a company that owns, operates or finances income-producing real estate and elects a special tax status. In exchange for distributing the bulk of its taxable income to shareholders (≈90%+ in the U.S.; Canadian REITs operate under similar flow-through trust rules), the entity pays little or no corporate tax. That pass-through structure is why REITs exist and why they pay high distributions: income that would otherwise be taxed at the corporate level is pushed out to unitholders.

How REITs make money: primarily by collecting rent from tenants on the properties they own (equity REITs), or by earning interest on real-estate loans and mortgage securities (mortgage REITs). They grow through rent escalations, higher occupancy, development, redevelopment, and acquisitions funded by issuing equity and debt.

Three ways to own them

Publicly traded REITs trade on exchanges — liquid, transparent, daily-priced, but volatile like stocks. Private / non-traded REITs are sold by brokers or sponsors — illiquid, often high-fee, valued infrequently, with redemption gates (the source of many investor complaints). REIT ETFs/mutual funds hold baskets of public REITs — instant diversification, low cost, and the right default for nearly all retail investors.

REITs are routinely compared with three things: bonds (because of the high, fairly steady income), dividend stocks (because they are equities that pay a lot), and direct real estate ownership (because the underlying assets are buildings). They sit between these — equity-like volatility, bond-like income, real-asset economics.

SECTION 3How to Analyze REITs — A Practical Framework

Forget P/E ratios — depreciation makes GAAP earnings nearly meaningless for property companies. REITs are analyzed on cash flow (FFO/AFFO), balance sheet, distribution safety and asset quality. Here is a five-lens checklist.

1 · Valuation

  • Price/FFO & Price/AFFO — the REIT equivalent of P/E. Compare to the REIT's own history and to sector peers, not across sectors (a data-center REIT deserves a higher multiple than an office REIT).
  • Implied cap rate — net operating income ÷ implied property value from the share price. A high implied cap rate vs private-market cap rates means the stock is cheap relative to the bricks.
  • NAV discount/premium — listed REITs in 2026 broadly trade at discounts to NAV, a setup that has historically preceded good forward returns.
  • Dividend yield vs its own historical range — a yield well above the 5-year average flags either value or distress; decide which.
  • REIT yield spread vs the 10-yr Treasury / GoC bond — the single most important macro gauge. Today the U.S. equity-REIT yield (~4.0%) sits below the 10-yr Treasury (4.48%), a historically tight/negative spread that partly explains weak recent REIT performance.

2 · Operating quality

Same-property NOI growth (organic pricing power), occupancy, rent growth (and mark-to-market on expiring leases), weighted-average lease duration, tenant credit quality, geographic diversification, and sector supply/demand balance. A REIT with 4–5% same-store NOI growth and rising occupancy is compounding; one renewing leases below expiring rents (much of office) is shrinking.

3 · Balance sheet

This is where REITs live or die in a high-rate world. Watch Debt/EBITDA (≤6x is conservative; the sector average debt ratio is ~32.5%), interest coverage (sector ~4.1x), the fixed- vs floating-rate mix (floating is dangerous now), weighted-average debt maturity and the near-term refinancing wall, the credit rating, and liquidity / access to capital. Well-capitalized REITs with long, fixed, laddered debt have a structural edge over levered, floating-rate peers.

4 · Distribution safety

AFFO payout ratio is the key number — under ~80% is comfortable for most sectors; above ~95% (as with some high-yield Canadian retail names) leaves no cushion. Look at distribution growth history, free-cash-flow coverage, and the realistic risk of a cut. A 9% yield with a 100%+ payout is a warning, not a gift.

5 · Growth drivers & macro sensitivity

Growth: organic rent escalators, development/redevelopment pipeline, accretive acquisitions, and the ability to issue equity above NAV. Macro: interest rates and inflation (dominant), credit spreads, GDP/employment, plus secular forces — e-commerce (industrial ↑), the digital economy and AI/data-center demand (towers & data centers ↑), work-from-home (office ↓), demographics/aging (healthcare & senior housing ↑), and housing affordability (apartments & single-family rental).

SECTION 4Major REIT Sectors

Real estate is not one asset — it is a dozen businesses with very different economics. Below, each sector's drivers, outlook and suitability, followed by a ranking table. Historical data on 2024 returns and current yields are from Nareit; growth/quality judgments are opinion.

Residential / Apartments

Owns multifamily buildings; revenue from rent on short (1-yr) leases that reprice with the market — giving good inflation pass-through. Demand is tied to jobs, household formation and housing affordability (expensive ownership keeps renters renting). Moderately defensive, moderately rate-sensitive. Suitable for income + moderate growth.

Industrial / Logistics

Warehouses and distribution centers — the physical backbone of e-commerce. A structural long-term winner with strong rent growth, though 2023–24 saw a cyclical cooldown after a torrid post-COVID run (the sector was down ~18% in 2024 as supply caught up). Suitable for growth-oriented investors; best-in-class operator is Prologis.

Retail / Shopping Centers

Split it in two. Grocery-anchored / open-air centers have proven resilient and well-occupied. Class-B malls are in secular decline, though top-tier malls (Simon) have recovered. Net-lease "free-standing" retail (Realty Income) is a bond-like income sub-sector. Higher yields (~5%), cyclical, e-commerce-pressured. Income-focused with care.

Office

The most structurally challenged sector. Work-from-home permanently lowered demand; older buildings face rising vacancy, falling rents and heavy refinancing risk. 2024 produced a sharp relief rally (+21%) off deeply depressed levels, but the long-term outlook is poor outside trophy assets. Avoid / underweight for most long-term investors; note the sector's ~5.1% yield reflects risk, not value.

Data Centers

The marquee growth sector — server "warehouses" powering cloud computing and the AI build-out. Very strong demand, low current yield (~2.4%, the lowest of any sector) because investors pay up for growth. Capital-intensive and power-constrained, but the secular tailwind is the strongest in real estate. Growth. Leaders: Equinix, Digital Realty.

Cell Towers / Infrastructure

Owns the towers carriers lease for wireless networks; inflation-linked, multi-tenant, contractual revenue with very high margins. A long-term compounder that de-rated sharply in 2022–24 on rates and slowing carrier spend (down ~14% in 2024). Growth + quality; American Tower, Crown Castle.

Healthcare & Senior Housing

Medical office, hospitals, lab/life-science space, and senior-living facilities. Demographics (aging population) are a powerful multi-decade tailwind; senior housing in particular is in a strong recovery (+24% in 2024). Mixed rate sensitivity. Income + demographic growth; Welltower is the bellwether.

Self-Storage

Small-box storage with very low operating costs, short leases (monthly pricing power) and recession resilience ("the 4 D's" — death, divorce, dislocation, downsizing keep demand steady). A long-run outperformer digesting a post-COVID demand normalization. Defensive growth; Public Storage, Extra Space.

Hotels / Lodging

Effectively re-prices rooms nightly — maximum inflation sensitivity but also maximum cyclicality and the deepest drawdowns in recessions. No lease protection. Cyclical; for risk-tolerant total-return investors only.

Single-Family Rental

Scattered or build-to-rent houses; benefits from unaffordable home ownership keeping families renting. Newer, smaller sub-sector with steady demand. Income + moderate growth.

Timberland

Owns forests; revenue from harvesting lumber and land. A genuine inflation/real-asset hedge but commodity-cyclical and tied to housing starts (down ~16% in 2024). Niche diversifier.

Mortgage REITs

Do not own buildings — they own mortgages with heavy leverage, earning a spread. Headline yields are enormous (~13%) but total returns have been poor and volatile (roughly flat — about +0.3%/yr over 20 years), with repeated distribution cuts. High-yield trap. Avoid for most long-term investors.

Diversified REITs

Own a mix of property types under one roof. Often trade at a discount (a "conglomerate" discount) and yield more (~6%), but the mix can dilute the best sectors. Case-by-case.

Volatility, drawdowns and correlation

Historical data REITs are more volatile than the S&P 500, not less. Annualized volatility runs roughly 18–22% vs ~15–16% for the S&P. Maximum drawdowns have been brutal: about −68% in the 2007–09 financial crisis (real estate was the epicentre) and −25% in 2022 as rates spiked. Correlation to the S&P 500 is high (~0.6–0.7) — so REITs diversify partially, not fully — while correlation to bonds is modest and has risen in the rate-driven era. The yield is the consolation for the volatility.

So — have REITs outperformed the S&P 500 over the long run?

No, not on the whole. Over 10 years (−6.7%/yr gap), 20 years (−3.4%/yr) and 30 years (−0.9%/yr), the S&P 500 won. REITs' headline 25-year victory (9.5% vs 7.9%) exists only because that window begins in 2000, capturing the dot-com collapse that crushed the tech-heavy S&P while REITs were cheap. Shift the start date a few years and the REIT win disappears. The durable conclusion: REITs have provided income and a different return stream, not long-run outperformance. They are a diversifier, not an alpha engine.

When are REITs better than a rental property for a retail investor?

Opinion. For the typical retail investor — especially an ETF-focused, diversified, total-return investor — REITs are the better default: they deliver real-estate exposure with instant diversification, professional management, liquidity, no leverage you have to personally guarantee, and no operational burden. Direct ownership wins only for those who specifically want (a) the amplifying power of mortgage leverage, (b) hands-on control and the ability to add value, (c) the tax shelter of depreciation against rental income, and (d) are willing to accept concentration and illiquidity. For a buy-and-hold investor who already owns broad equities, a REIT ETF adds the asset class without turning real estate into a second job.

SECTION 8REITs in a Portfolio

REITs wear several hats, and which one matters depends on the investor:

  • As income assets — a ~4% (U.S.) to ~5%+ (Canada) yield, well above the S&P 500's ~1.2%, much of it paid monthly by Canadian REITs.
  • As equity assets — they are stocks, with equity-like volatility and drawdowns; they are not a bond substitute despite the income.
  • As inflation-sensitive / real assets — rents and property values tend to rise with inflation, especially in short-lease sectors (apartments, hotels, storage).
  • As a partial diversifier — ~0.6–0.7 correlation to the S&P means they zig somewhat differently, but not dramatically.

The bond-substitute trap: investors reaching for REITs "instead of bonds" because of the yield get burned in selloffs — in 2022 both fell, and REITs fell far more. Treat REITs as the high-income, real-asset sleeve of your equities, not as ballast.

SECTION 9Canadian Retail Investor Considerations

For a Canadian, the REIT decision is dominated by tax and account placement, then currency, then product.

The withholding-tax rule that drives everything

Key cross-border rule. U.S. REIT distributions paid to a Canadian face a 15% U.S. withholding tax — except in an RRSP/RRIF, where the Canada–U.S. tax treaty waives it for U.S.-listed ETFs holding U.S. securities. In a TFSA, FHSA or RESP the 15% is withheld and is not recoverable (no foreign tax credit in a tax-free account). In a taxable account it is withheld but you can claim a foreign tax credit. Conclusion: hold U.S.-listed U.S. REIT ETFs (like VNQ) in your RRSP.

Distribution taxation in a taxable account

Canadian REIT distributions are not "eligible dividends" — they are a blend of other income (taxed at your full marginal rate), return of capital (not taxed now; lowers your cost base, deferring tax to sale), capital gains (half-taxed) and foreign income. The high ordinary-income component makes REITs tax-inefficient in a non-registered account — another reason to shelter them.

Currency

Current: USD/CAD ≈ 1.40. A Canadian buying U.S. REIT ETFs takes on USD exposure. Opinion: for a long-term diversified investor I would not bother hedging — currency-hedged versions cost more and add tracking error, and unhedged USD exposure is itself a diversifier for a Canadian whose income and home are in CAD.

SECTION 10U.S. Retail Investor Considerations

For a U.S. investor the cross-border tax issue disappears, and the decision reduces to cost, breadth, and account placement.

The U.S. tax point that matters most

REIT distributions are generally non-qualified — taxed as ordinary income, not at the lower qualified-dividend rate. (A portion may qualify for the 20% QBI deduction through 2025 law, and some is return of capital or capital gain.) The practical takeaway: hold REITs in a tax-advantaged account — Traditional IRA, Roth IRA or 401(k) — wherever possible. A Roth is ideal: the high, ordinary-income yield then compounds and is withdrawn tax-free. State tax adds to the case for sheltering. ETFs are also more tax-efficient and far cheaper than most actively managed real-estate mutual funds.

Current valuation. The rate cycle has turned: short rates have eased toward ~3.7% (1-month bill) while the 10-year sits at 4.48%, leaving a normal, modestly upward-sloping curve. Both the Fed and Bank of Canada have moved off their peak-restrictive stance, which historically helps REITs — but long yields remain elevated, so the tailwind is gentle, not a gale.

Key readings: credit spreads are tight (high-yield steady), supportive of refinancing. Cap rates have largely reset higher and appear to be stabilizing. Listed REITs trade at discounts to NAV and to lagging private-market real-estate valuations — the classic setup where public markets have already repriced and private marks are catching down. Sector-by-sector: office remains stressed (though bouncing off lows), industrial is normalizing after oversupply, data centers are booming on AI/cloud demand, apartment rent growth has cooled to low-single-digits, retail (grocery-anchored) is healthy, and healthcare/senior housing is in a clear up-cycle.

Are REITs cheap, fair, or expensive today?

Opinion: fairly-to-attractively valued, but not a screaming bargain. On NAV discounts and dividend yields above their own history, REITs look cheap. But the REIT yield (~4%) sits below the 10-year Treasury (4.48%) — a negative yield spread that is historically unusual and caps how cheap they really are versus risk-free income. The setup favours quality, lower-leverage, growth-sector REITs over a blanket bet. If long rates fall from here, REITs should re-rate; if they stay high, returns lean on income and earnings growth.

SECTION 13Recommended REIT Strategy

Conservative income investor

Prioritize stable distributions and low leverage. Use a diversified REIT ETF as the whole position; avoid mortgage REITs and weak office. Canada: ZRE (equal-weight reduces single-name risk) or VRE in a TFSA. U.S.: SCHH/USRT in an IRA. Target the low end of the allocation range (5–10%).

Balanced long-term investor (your profile)

A broad, low-cost REIT ETF core, diversified across the U.S. and Canada, sized at 5–10% of the portfolio. Favour low fees and correct account placement above all. This is the "set it and forget it" approach the data supports.

Growth-oriented investor

Tilt toward the structural winners — data centers, towers, industrial, self-storage, residential — and underweight traditional retail and office. Achievable via a broad U.S. ETF (which already overweights these) plus optional single-name satellites (PLD, AMT, EQIX, WELL, PSA). Keep total REITs ≤10%.

SECTION 14Products to Avoid or Treat Carefully

High yield does not equal good value.

The single most common REIT mistake is buying the biggest yield. A high distribution yield usually reflects high risk — leverage, a stressed sector, or an unsustainable payout the market expects to be cut. Total return, cost, and balance-sheet safety beat headline yield every time.

  • Mortgage-REIT ETFs (REM, MORT): ~10% yields, but ~0% real total return over 20 years, heavy leverage, repeated dividend cuts. Not buy-and-hold assets.
  • Non-traded / private REITs: high commissions, illiquidity, redemption gates (several have frozen withdrawals), and opaque valuations. Generally avoid.
  • High-fee actively managed real-estate mutual funds: rarely beat a 0.07% index ETF after fees.
  • Leveraged / closed-end real-estate funds: borrow to boost yield; magnify drawdowns and often trade at volatile premiums/discounts.
  • Covered-call REIT ETFs: boost current income by selling away upside — fine if you only want cash flow, but they structurally cap total return, the wrong trade for a long-term compounder.
  • Concentrated office REITs & high-payout names: AFFO payouts above ~95% (e.g. some Canadian retail names) and floating-rate-heavy balance sheets carry real distribution-cut risk.
  • U.S. REIT ETFs in a TFSA/FHSA: not a "bad product," but a costly placement error — the 15% withholding is lost permanently.

SECTION 15Final Conclusion

Are REITs worth owning? Yes — as a modest, deliberately-placed sleeve for income, diversification and real-asset/inflation exposure. Not as a bet to beat the S&P 500, which the 10-, 20- and 30-year record does not support.

Most attractive sectors: data centers, industrial/logistics, cell towers, self-storage, and senior housing. Underweight or avoid: commodity office, Class-B malls, and mortgage REITs.

Canadian investors: use both, by account. Hold a broad U.S. REIT ETF (VNQ/SCHH) in the RRSP for sector breadth and treaty-protected income, and optionally a Canadian-listed ETF (VRE/ZRE) in the TFSA for home-currency cash flow. Don't go Canada-only — you'd miss the data-center and tower growth entirely. U.S. investors: a single low-cost broad ETF (SCHH/USRT/VNQ) in a Roth or IRA does ~95% of the job.

How much? 5–15% of the portfolio for most, lower if you already own a home and cannot shelter the income, higher only for a deliberate real-asset tilt.

For most long-term retail investors, the best REIT approach is a small (5–10%) allocation to one or two low-cost, broadly diversified REIT ETFs — a U.S. broad fund (VNQ/SCHH) in a tax-sheltered account as the core, optionally complemented by a domestic-listed fund — held for income, diversification and inflation exposure rather than for beating the S&P 500, with sector quality (data centers, industrial, towers, storage) favoured over chasing the highest yield.
