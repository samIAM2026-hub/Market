1. The regime in one page

Pull the 16 notes together and a single, coherent macro picture emerges rather than a set of unrelated headlines:

  • Inflation has re-accelerated and the Fed is talking hikes, not cuts. June CPI at 3.5% (core 2.6%), a new hawkish Chair (Warsh) calling inflation a "choice," and markets pricing at least one hike this year. This is a higher-for-longer, hike-risk world — the opposite of a soft-landing easing cycle.
  • A live geopolitical oil shock. The Iran/US war has killed the Strait of Hormuz agreement; oil is spiking, and ~1/5 of the world's oil transits Hormuz. This is simultaneously inflationary and growth-negative — the textbook stagflation channel — and it feeds straight back into the Fed problem.
  • The AI/mega-cap moat is being questioned for the first time in 18 months. Moonshot's open-weight Kimi-K3, the China memory IPOs (CXMT/YMTC), and capex-ROI doubts pushed the SOX into a ~20% bear market from its 22 June peak, with the Nasdaq-100 on track for its worst July in 20+ years.
  • Valuations leave no cushion. S&P 500 at ~29× trailing GAAP (only ~22× on adjusted, flattered 2026 numbers), IG credit spreads near post-2008 tights, and private markets stretched (SpaceX alone shed ~$1T from its June peak).
  • But the plumbing is healthy, and breadth is fine. Banks posted ~24% ROEs and record trading, credit losses are low, sentiment ticked up, and the equal-weight S&P just made an all-time high while the Dow outperforms. This is a rotation, not (yet) a systemic break.

Net: the easy, concentrated, long-duration-everything trade is over. The market is repricing from "AI + disinflation + Fed put" to "sticky inflation + oil + AI-ROI scrutiny + no Fed put." That transition is where the opportunity and the risk both live.

2. Theme-by-theme synthesis

2.1 · Inflation & the Fed — higher-for-longer, with hike risk

Ref: Fed Talks Tough · Global Rates

The single most important macro fact in the vault: inflation is at 3.5% and the policy debate is about hikes, not cuts. The June print (down from 4.2%) was flattered by gasoline — which the Hormuz oil shock now threatens to reverse. The FOMC "straw count" leans to a hold at 28–29 July, but Logan is openly hawkish and the committee wants "several months" of good data. Front-end (2yr ~4.14%) is anchored by hike risk; the 10yr (~4.55%) is caught between safe-haven demand and supply/inflation pressure. Implication: duration is not your friend, and any oil-driven CPI re-acceleration puts a hike genuinely in play.

2.2 · Geopolitics — Iran/Hormuz is a macro variable, not a sidebar

Ref: US Servicemembers Killed · Global Rates

The collapse of the Hormuz MOU and the first US combat deaths since the April ceasefire mean escalation risk is rising, not fading. Oil is already higher; a genuine Hormuz disruption is the fat left tail for the whole complex because it forces the Fed's hand (inflation) while hurting growth (energy tax). This is the mechanism that could turn an orderly rotation into a stagflationary risk-off. Gold firm, USD bid, energy structurally supported.

2.3 · The AI/chip narrative crack — real, but historically survivable

Ref: Tech Selloff · Kimi-K3 · China Memory IPOs · Software Shakeout · Markets Wrap

Three distinct pressures hit the AI trade at once: (1) competitive — Kimi-K3 and Chinese open-weight models questioning the US moat; (2) supply — CXMT/YMTC memory capacity threatening to end the DRAM/NAND upcycle; (3) business-model — AI eroding seat-based SaaS margins and commoditizing "intelligence" itself. The counter-argument: every prior "AI scare" (DeepSeek, Jan 2025) expanded adoption rather than collapsing it. Base case: cheaper intelligence broadens the pie and value migrates up the stack (data/security platforms, embedded incumbents like MSFT/SAP) — but the pricing of that outcome was too rich, so a de-rating of the most crowded semis/mega-cap names is healthy, not necessarily terminal.

2.4 · Valuation & positioning — no margin of safety, in equities or credit

Ref: S&P Fine Print · Corporate Bonds · New VC Playbook

Both public and private markets are priced for perfection. The S&P is ~29× trailing GAAP and only looks "cheap" if you accept non-GAAP earnings that exclude real costs. Long IG corporate yields near 6% look attractive in isolation, but the spread over short-dated paper (~50bps) is near record tights — you are not paid to take duration, credit and AI-cycle risk for decades. Private markets echo the same stretch (Anthropic at high-hundreds-of-billions; SpaceX −$1T in a month). Common thread: elevated yields everywhere mean you don't have to reach — cash and the front end pay you to wait.

2.5 · Market internals — this is a rotation, and it's orderly

Ref: Tech Selloff · Berkshire

The tell that this is a repricing and not a crash: equal-weight S&P at an all-time high, the Dow outperforming, and leadership in healthcare, financials, energy, utilities. Even Berkshire — ~$400B cash, accelerating buybacks, buying Alphabet — is positioned defensively and value-first. When the market's most patient capital is hoarding cash and the index is rotating rather than collapsing, the message is reduce concentration and up-quality, not de-gross everything.

2.6 · Banks — mint nominal profits, but late-cycle

Ref: Big Banks Goldilocks · Analysts Raise Targets

Q2 was "Goldilocks": ~24% ROE at JPM/GS, equity-trading revenue up 69–86%, low credit losses, a wave of upgrades. But NIMs are thin, spreads tight, and the piece itself warns this is "as good as it gets," with capital-markets conditions possibly at a cyclical peak. Banks screen well on ROE-vs-valuation and are a natural home for rotation flows, but this is a trade for balance-sheet quality (JPM) into a late cycle, not a blank-check re-rating.

5. Conclusion — the trade

The vault, read as a whole, describes a hand-off from one macro regime to another: from disinflation + AI exceptionalism + Fed put to sticky inflation + oil geopolitics + AI-ROI scrutiny + a Fed that is out of the rescue business. Crucially, the economy's nominal engine is still strong (bank ROEs, low credit losses, firm sentiment), so this is a repricing, not a recession call — which is exactly why the market is rotating (equal-weight at highs) rather than crashing.

Cut concentration and duration; get paid to wait; own the rotation and the oil hedge.

Concretely: reduce crowded mega-cap tech/semis and long-duration IG credit where you are not compensated; overweight value, defensives, energy and quality banks absorbing the rotation; keep the front end and cash working as a funded, positively-carrying "wait" position; hold gold and energy as the Hormuz/stagflation hedge; and use Korea "value-up" as an idiosyncratic, uncorrelated deep-value long. The single variable that flips this from constructive-rotation to defensive-risk-off is oil via Hormuz — that is the chart to watch every morning, because it is the one that can force the Fed's hand.

The through-line across every note is the same: you are no longer paid to reach. Yields are high across the stack, valuations are rich, and the marginal buyer of risk is being asked to accept a lot of tail — geopolitical, inflation, AI-ROI, supply — for very little incremental spread or earnings yield. In that world, the highest-expected-value posture is patient, up-quality and diversified away from the crowded trade, which is precisely how the market's most patient capital is already positioned.
